1         Background

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) exerts a tremendous and growing burden on patients, the society, and the Canadian economy. It is now responsible for more hospitalizations than any other chronic disease in Canada, and it is estimated that its direct medical costs will soar to $7.33billion per year by 2035[1]. Many of COPD risk factors, such as smoking and occupational hazards are modifiable, and evidence is growing that treatment at early stage can change the course of the disease. In addition, there is a growing interest in developing biomarkers for COPD that can help with a timely diagnosis of the disease itself and its exacerbations. Together these indicate tremendous opportunity in reducing the burden of COPD by adopting evidence-informed diagnosis and management strategies especially at early stages. Yet, currently, such opportunities are lost as around 35% of Canadians with COPD remain undiagnosed[2]. There are substantial efforts at the national and international level by detecting COPD at early stages and providing evidence-based treatment.

Ultimately, evaluating the efficiency of such programs hinges upon valid predictions of the future burden of COPD under various policy scenarios. Such evaluations require predicting long-term outcomes beyond the follow-up time of empirical studies, synthesizing evidence from disparate sources, and consideration of uncertainty in the evidence. Mathematical simulation models provide powerful analytical tools for objective evaluation of such policies[3].

Modules are conceptually distinct units. Operationally, modules correspond to separate code files that related to each other but are otherwise conceptually independent. The model consists of the following modules

2.1.1  Global parameters

Time horizon: This is the time horizon of the model. The default is set at 20 years.

 

Discount values for costs and QALYs: This is the extent to which future rates of payoffs (costs and QALYs) are discounted. The default value is 3%. This means compared to year t, payoffs at year t+1 are given 3% lower value.

 

 

 

2.1.2  Demographic module

This module will eventually use data from Canadian Community Health Survey and Canadian study of Obstructive Lung Disease (CanCOLD).

Currently, this module allows the investigator to set a specific sex and age distribution for the baseline population. Sex is determined by the proportion of female, and age is determined by specifying a mean and standard deviation.

agent$p_female: proportion of female in the population (default=50%).

agent$age_at_baseline_mu: Mean age at baseline

agent$age_at_baseline_sd: Standard deviation (SD) of age at baseline

2.1.3  Smoking module

The smoking module is responsible for modeling the smoking behaviour in individuals. Note that the smoking history at baseline (pack-years) is handled in the demographic module.

The events associated with smoking are handled through smoking_change event.

smoking$inc_by_age_sex: Annual sex- and age-specific rate of smoking incidence in never-smokers. This value is taken from the BOLD model [REF].

smoking$ces_by_age_sex: Annual sex- and age-specific rate of smoking cessation in current smokers. This value is taken from the BOLD model [REF].

smoking$rel_by_age_sex: Annual sex- and age-specific rate of smoking relapse in ex-smokers. This value is taken from the BOLD model [REF].

 

2.1.4  Lung Function (disease progression) module

Lung function at baseline:

We rely on the patient-level data of the nationally representative COLD and the Canadian Cohort of Obstructive Lung Disease (CanCOLD) studies [4][5]. Both studies are population-based with multi-stage sampling to ensure representativeness. We analyze these data to obtain our own equations, on joint estimation of FEV1 and FVC, required for properly characterizing FEV1/FVC as it defines COPD [Table]. The equations are of the form:

FEV1= β0+β1.X1+β2.X2+ β3.X3+ +ε

COLD/CanCOLD equation

with X representing covariates (sex, age at baseline, symptoms, smoking history, body metrics); the correlation between ε and ε' is estimated from the covariance matrix of regression residuals. This will allow us to assign to each individual (generated in Step 1) FEV1 and FVC values.

lung_function$fev1_0_betas: Is the regression coefficients for baseline FEV1. Note that it is currently based on an intercept, sex and age.

lung_function$fev1_0_sd: SD of error term (ε)

 

Lung function decline over time:

The rate of FEV1 decline will follow our longitudinal prediction equations derived from dedicated analysis of LHS data. The risk prediction equations for FEV1 is of type

FEV1(t)= FEV1(t0)+(t- t0).(β1.X1+β2.X2+ +u1.Z1+ u 2.Z2+ ) +ε LHS equation

with FEV1(t) being FEV1 at time t (>t0), FEV1(t0) being the FEV1 at the time of screening/case detection, and ε representing an independent, normally distributed error term. The term in parenthesis is the individual-specific rate of decline. Xs and Zs are, respectively, the set of fixed-effect and random-effect covariates.

 

lung_function$dfev1_betas: Is the regression coefficients of the above equation. It currently includes intercept, sex, baseline age (age0), baseline FEV1 (fev1_0), smoking status (smoking), and time since entry in the model (time). Currently, the intercept and time variables are modeled as random-effects.

lung_function$dfev1_re_sds: SD of two random-effect terms (intercept and time) for FEV1 slope.

lung_function$dfev1_re_rho: Correlation coefficient for the two random-effect terms (intercept and time) for FEV1 slope.

 

2.1.5  Exacerbation module

We have used the event-based definition of exacerbations. Exacerbations are classified into mild, moderate, and severe.

exacerbation$exac_betas[0:intercept,1:sex,2:age,3:pred_fev1]

This models the instantaneous hazard (rate) of exacerbation given individuals characteristics. This is a random-effects equation with the coefficient for intercept varying across individuals (see next parameter).

exacerbation$exac_re_var

It is known that individuals vary in their rate of exacerbation even after controlling for predictors of exacerbations. This is modeled by assigning a random-effect term to the intercept.

 

exacerbation$p_moderate_severe[0:moderate,1:severe]

.

 

exacerbation$exac_end_rate[mild, moderate, severe]

This parameter is responsible for determining the end of an exacerbation period. This is a Poisson process, and corresponds to the average length of stay of exacerbations through Rate=365/LOS.

exacerbation$p_death[mild, moderate, severe]

This is the probability of death due to an exacerbation, stratified according to the rate of exacerbation. Currently, mortality is assumed to occur at the beginning of exacerbation.
